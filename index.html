About

I am a PhD candidate in the School of Mechanical Engineering at Purdue University, advised by Prof. Partha P. Mukherjee in the Energy and Transport Science Lab. My dissertation develops a mesoscale understanding of instability at solid-state electrochemical interfaces, with a defense expected in August 2026.

Before Purdue, I completed a B.Tech and M.Tech dual degree in Chemical Engineering at IIT Kharagpur, where my thesis on the mesoscale origin of voids in metal electrodes seeded my current research direction. My work is supported by the DOE Basic Energy Sciences MUSIC EFRC and the NSF.

I care about physics that lives at the mesoscale. Atomistic detail matters, continuum behavior matters, and the most interesting failure modes in solid-state batteries emerge in the space between the two.

Research Highlights

Solid-state batteries promise safer, denser energy storage, but the lithium metal and solid electrolyte interface is their weakest link. During stripping, vacancies injected at the interface condense into voids. Voids concentrate current, current drives dendrites, and dendrites kill cells. My research builds predictive, physics-based models of this cascade. Full project details live on the Research page.

Kinetic Monte Carlo

Void formation and evolution

Lattice-based kMC simulations that resolve vacancy injection, surface diffusion, and void coalescence at the Li and solid electrolyte interface, mapping the conditions under which morphological instability emerges.

Electro-chemo-mechanics

Stress-coupled interface dynamics

A coupled kMC and FFT micromechanics framework in which local stress states modify activation barriers, capturing how plasticity and creep mediate single void growth under stack pressure.

Mesoscale physics

Interfacial stability maps

Phase maps linking operating conditions, transport properties, and microstructure to interface stability, including the role of temperature, vacancy diffusion, and secondary metal phases.
